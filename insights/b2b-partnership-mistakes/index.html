The research

Where partnerships break down

These three patterns appear consistently across industries, geographies, and company sizes.

01
Mistake 01

Choosing partners by location, not strategic fit

"We need a partner in Brazil" is a geographic goal, not a strategy. Companies that expand successfully define first: what values, business culture, and growth stage their ideal partner needs to have.

When executives lead with geography, they filter for proximity rather than compatibility — and proximity alone has never sustained a partnership.

Dissolution rate

78%

of partnerships with low strategic compatibility dissolve within the first 12 months — regardless of the market or industry.

"We spent 4 months negotiating with a company in the same sector. The partnership never happened — because no one checked strategic fit first."
02
Mistake 02

Starting negotiations without verifying credibility

Time is the scarcest resource in international expansion. Meetings with companies that have no verifiable track record cost weeks of negotiation — and sometimes much more.

The instinct to "just get on a call" feels efficient. It isn't. Basic due diligence, done before the first contact, changes everything.

Qualification cycle reduction

60%

Basic pre-contact due diligence reduces the qualification cycle by up to 60% — saving weeks of negotiation time per partner.

"Information about a company's partnership history and financial health could have saved us weeks. We had good faith. We didn't have enough information."
03
Mistake 03

Relying on in-person networking as your only source

Trade shows, events, and conferences have real value. But they limit your reach to the circle of people already in the room — and create a powerful selection bias.

The best partnerships of 2024 and 2025 were formed asynchronously, based on data — not on who you happened to meet at a buffet.

Top partnerships formed

Async

The highest-value cross-border partnerships in 2024–25 were initiated through data-driven matching — not events or referrals.

"Every partner I considered came from the same events circuit. I was ignoring entire markets simply because they weren't in my network."
